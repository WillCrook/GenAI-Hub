What is changing?

From ABC traffic lights to Two Lanes

Bath now uses a simpler question to explain GenAI use in assessment: is this assessment Closed or Open to GenAI?

​

ABC was the traffic-light model

You may still hear Type A, B or C used informally. Those labels map onto the Two-Lane approach below.

​

Two Lanes are clearer

Closed means GenAI must not be used. Open means GenAI may be limited, optional or required depending on the brief.

​

You remain responsible

AI can support your thinking, but it must not replace your judgement, analysis or learning.

What this means for you

Read the assessment brief first. If it uses older ABC wording, translate it like this: Type A means Closed, Type B means Open with defined limits, and Type C means Open with GenAI integral or required.

Open Lane

AI may be allowed

In an Open Lane assessment, GenAI use is optional or integral, depending on the task.

What this can include

  • Coursework, reports and projects
  • Open-book work, including some remote open-book exams
  • Authentic tasks where AI use is expected in professional practice
  • Work that assesses judgement, critique, synthesis, source use or process

What this means for you

Use AI only in the ways the brief allows. If AI is required, show how you used it thoughtfully and critically.

Closed Lane

AI must not be used

In a Closed Lane assessment, GenAI tools are not permitted.

What this can include

  • Exams and invigilated work
  • In-person tests
  • Lab practicals where AI would interfere with what is being assessed
  • Foundational knowledge, fluency, threshold concepts or regulated competence

What this means for you

Using AI for revision is permitted where appropriate. However, during assessments where GenAI is not allowed, students must complete the work independently and must not rely on or reproduce AI-generated content.

ABC mapping

How the old labels fit the new lanes

Type A

Closed

GenAI is not permitted. The assessment is designed so AI should not be used.

Type B

Open, with limits

GenAI may be used only for defined assistive processes, such as planning, feedback or checking, where the brief allows it.

Type C

Open, integral

GenAI has an integral role and may be required. You still need to show your own decisions and critical judgement.

Responsible use

AI should support your thinking, not replace it

You are responsible for the work you submit, including any AI-assisted parts.

​

You decide

Use your own judgement about what to ask, what to accept and what to reject.

​

You check

Verify facts, references, data, calculations, code and claims before you rely on them.

​

You write

Your final submission should reflect your understanding, structure, evidence and voice.

​

You disclose

When asked, explain how AI was used so markers can understand your process.

Coursework

AI-use statements

For Type B and Type C coursework, your brief may ask for a short statement explaining how you used GenAI.

A useful statement usually says

  • Which tool you used
  • What you used it for
  • Which parts of the work it influenced
  • How you checked the output
  • How you changed or rejected suggestions
  • Any limits or mistakes you noticed

Example wording

I used a GenAI tool to generate possible essay structures and to ask for feedback on clarity. I checked all claims against academic sources and wrote the final analysis myself.

Student scenarios

Good and poor GenAI use

The same tool can be helpful or risky depending on the brief and how you use it.

Good use

Planning a report in a Type B assessment

You ask AI to suggest possible structures, compare those suggestions with the brief, choose your own structure, write the report yourself and mention this in your AI-use statement.

Why this works: AI supports the process, but your judgement and writing remain central.

Poor use

Submitting AI-written paragraphs as your answer

You paste the question into a chatbot, copy the response into your coursework and do not check the evidence, rewrite the argument or disclose the use.

Why this is risky: the work no longer clearly shows your own thinking.

Good use

Using AI in a Type C professional task

The brief asks you to use GenAI to produce and critique outputs. You keep notes on prompts, compare outputs, identify weaknesses and explain your decisions.

Why this works: the assessment is testing effective, ethical and critical AI use.

Poor use

Using AI during a Closed Lane task

An in-person test or lab practical says GenAI is not permitted, but you use a tool to generate an answer, calculation or explanation during the assessment.

Why this is risky: AI use is outside the conditions of the assessment.

Before using AI

Ask yourself...

​ Have I checked the assessment brief first?
​ Do I know whether this is Type A, B or C, or Closed or Open Lane?
​ Am I using AI only in the ways the brief allows?
​ Can I explain and defend the final work myself?
​ Have I checked facts, references, calculations and claims?
​ Have I included an AI-use statement if required?
If unsure

Ask before you use AI

If the brief is unclear, pause and ask your unit convenor, tutor or teaching team. It is normal to check before using AI in assessed work.

Send a simple question

"Can you confirm whether GenAI is allowed for this assessment, and whether I need to include an AI-use statement?"
